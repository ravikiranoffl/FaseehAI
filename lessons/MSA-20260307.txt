**Today's Focus: Plural Demonstrative Pronouns - 'These' and 'Those' for People and Things**

Yesterday, we mastered "this" and "that" for singular items. Today, we're taking a super exciting step into the plural world! We'll learn how to say "these" and "those," and there's a little twist for non-human plurals that's super important in Arabic!

**Notes & Explanation**

Alright, friend, let's learn how to point to more than one thing or person!

1.  **"These" for People (<AUDIO: هَؤُلَاءِ>):**
    *   When you want to say "these" and you're talking about **people** (like students, teachers, friends), you use <AUDIO: هَؤُلَاءِ> (hā'ulā'i). This pronoun is super versatile because it works for *both* masculine and feminine plurals of people!

2.  **"These" and "Those" for Things (The Special Rule!):**
    *   Now, here's the cool part: In Arabic, when you talk about **non-human plurals** (like houses, cars, books, trees, animals – anything that isn't a person), they are treated grammatically as if they were **feminine singular**!
    *   This means:
        *   For "these" (non-human plural, near), we actually **reuse** <AUDIO: هَذِهِ> (hādhāhi), our feminine singular "this"!
        *   For "those" (non-human plural, far), we **reuse** <AUDIO: تِلْكَ> (tilka), our feminine singular "that"!

    It sounds a bit odd at first ("this are houses"?), but this is a fundamental and common rule in Arabic, and you'll get used to it very quickly! The noun itself will be in its plural form, but the demonstrative pronoun acting on it will be feminine singular.

So, to sum up:
*   People (plural): Use <AUDIO: هَؤُلَاءِ> for "these."
*   Things (plural): Use <AUDIO: هَذِهِ> for "these" and <AUDIO: تِلْكَ> for "those."

You've got this! This rule is a cornerstone of Arabic grammar.

**Examples in Context**
Arabic: <AUDIO: هَؤُلَاءِ طُلَّابٌ.>
Transliteration: hā'ulā'i ṭullābun.
Meaning: These are students. (Here, <AUDIO: طُلَّابٌ> is a rational masculine plural.)

Arabic: <AUDIO: هَؤُلَاءِ مُعَلِّمَاتٌ.>
Transliteration: hā'ulā'i muʿallimātun.
Meaning: These are female teachers. (Here, <AUDIO: مُعَلِّمَاتٌ> is a rational feminine plural.)

Arabic: <AUDIO: هَذِهِ بُيُوتٌ.>
Transliteration: hādhihi buyūtun.
Meaning: These are houses. (Even though <AUDIO: بُيُوتٌ> is plural, it's non-human, so we use <AUDIO: هَذِهِ>.)

Arabic: <AUDIO: تِلْكَ كُتُبٌ.>
Transliteration: tilka kutubun.
Meaning: Those are books. (Similarly, <AUDIO: كُتُبٌ> is plural and non-human, so we use <AUDIO: تِلْكَ>.)
